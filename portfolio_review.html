What is being investigated. OatFi lends to small businesses through Order, an embedded lending partner. Obligors repay Order directly on the invoice due date. Order is contractually required to pass those funds to OatFi immediately. OatFi's own maturity date sits roughly 60 days after the partner due date as a grace period, and Order must stop lending to any obligor who is more than 45 days past due.

This investigation examines whether Order has been collecting repayments from obligors on time but holding those funds in its own accounts — earning interest on OatFi's capital — before remitting close to OatFi's maturity deadline. It also examines whether Order continued originating new loans to obligors who were already past the day-45 cutoff.
Finding summary

Before Q1 2024, Order forwarded repayments promptly — funds arrived around the partner due date, roughly 56 days before OatFi's own maturity deadline, and fewer than 0.02% of loans fell in the 45–60 day grace period window. This is the expected baseline for a well-behaved servicer.

From April 2024 onwards, 10.5% of paid loans arrived in the 45–60 day window — between the partner's day-45 cutoff and OatFi's day-60 maturity. These loans were not past due at OatFi, but they arrived in the final 15 days of our window rather than promptly at the partner due date. The worst month was 2025-09-01 at 52.4%. This is consistent with Order collecting from obligors on time but holding the funds before remitting.

296 obligors received new loans from Order while a prior loan was already past the day-45 cutoff and unpaid to OatFi — totaling $310.8M in new originations. This confirms the cutoff is not being enforced and raises the possibility that new loan proceeds are being used to manufacture repayments on old ones.

Conservative float income at 5% annual on $45.3M principal held for an average of 52.4 days: $325K. At prevailing SOFR+ rates during the period the likely range is $325K–$813K.

Signal 2 — payment timing shift: where payoffs land relative to the partner due date
What this chart shows. Each bar counts how many loans were paid off at a given number of days relative to the partner due date, from 60 days early to 60 days late. The green bars (left of zero) are loans paid before the due date. DPD=0 is exactly on the due date — the correct behavior. The dark red shaded region (DPD 45–60) is the suspicious window.
