What is being investigated. Instead of passing repayments through immediately, Order may be collecting funds from obligors and holding them in its own accounts — earning interest on OatFi's capital — before remitting just before OatFi's maturity deadline. This is called remittance float or servicer commingling. In addition Order continued lending to obligors who were already past the day-45 cutoff, which could potentially allow the partner to use new loan proceeds to manufacture repayments on old ones.
Finding summary

What this chart shows. Each bar represents one calendar month. The height shows what percentage of that month's loan payoffs arrived between 45 and 60 days after the partner's own invoice due date. Before Q2-2024 this was essentially zero — the baseline for a healthy servicer. Signal 2 — DPD=0 spike: payments clustering on the exact due date
What this chart shows. Each bar counts how many loan payoffs arrived at a given number of days relative to the partner due date, from 5 days early (DPD -5) to 5 days late (DPD +5). Payments on the exact due date (DPD=0, shown in green) are the expected, correct behavior — this is when the invoice is due. The spike at DPD=0 therefore tells us obligors are paying on time. What is suspicious is the volume of payments arriving in the 45–60 day window (Signal 1) — those are funds the partner held well past the due date before remitting. The DPD=0 concentration here simply confirms that when the partner does remit on time, it does so in large batches on the exact due date rather than passing individual payments through as they are collected.

Signal 4 — cutoff enforcement: new loans issued while a prior loan was already 45+ days past due and unpaid to OatFi
What this means. Order's contract requires it to stop issuing new loans to an obligor once that obligor is 45 or more days past due on an existing loan. This table shows obligors who received a new loan from Order during the period when a prior loan was already past that cutoff and still unpaid to OatFi.

This has two serious implications. First, it confirms the day-45 cutoff is not being enforced — this is systematic across 294 obligors, not isolated exceptions. Second, when a new loan is originated to an already-delinquent obligor, the new loan proceeds can be used to settle the old one. This means some of OatFi's "repayments" may not reflect genuine collections from obligors but rather fresh capital recycled through new originations — a mechanism that masks the true delinquency rate and inflates repayment performance.

Max DPD at reborrow shows how many days past the partner due date the old loan was when the new one was originated. Any value above 45 is a direct breach of the cutoff policy.
