What is being investigated. OatFi lends to small businesses through Order, an embedded lending partner. Obligors repay Order directly on the invoice due date. Order is contractually required to pass those funds to OatFi immediately. OatFi's own maturity date sits roughly 60 days after the partner due date as a grace period, and Order must stop lending to any obligor who is more than 45 days past due.

This investigation examines whether Order has been collecting repayments from obligors on time but holding those funds in its own accounts — earning interest on OatFi's capital — before remitting close to OatFi's maturity deadline. It also examines whether Order continued originating new loans to obligors who were already past the day-45 cutoff.
Finding summary

Before Q3 2025, Order forwarded repayments promptly — funds arrived around the partner due date, roughly 56 days before OatFi's own maturity deadline, and fewer than 0.02% of loans fell in the 45–60 day grace period window. This is the expected baseline for a well-behaved servicer.

From September 2025 onwards, 23.8% of paid loans arrived in the 45–60 day window — between the partner's day-45 cutoff and OatFi's day-60 maturity. These loans were not past due at OatFi, but they arrived in the final 15 days of our window rather than promptly at the partner due date. The worst month was 2025-09-01 at 52.4%. This is consistent with Order collecting from obligors on time but holding the funds before remitting.

279 obligors received new loans from Order while a prior loan was already past the day-45 cutoff and unpaid to OatFi — totaling $91.7M in new originations. This confirms the cutoff is not being enforced and raises the possibility that new loan proceeds are being used to manufacture repayments on old ones.

Conservative float income at 5% annual on $40.6M principal held for an average of 52.5 days: $292K. At prevailing SOFR+ rates during the period the likely range is $292K–$730K.

Definition: A reborrow is a new origination to an org on a date when that org already had an outstanding loan (curr_upb > $10) that was more than 45 days past its due date. The loan ledger is used to determine the exact state of each loan on each origination date — if any prior loan was 45+ DPD on that date, any new origination to that org on that same date is a violation.
Notable exclusion — structural late-payment obligor requiring separate review
One obligor excluded from Signal 4 warrants independent scrutiny. Verano Holdings Corp (org ID 65d8cbf57e5e5c005b20cd6d) originated approximately $17M across 2,756 loans between March 2024 and February 2026. Every single loan paid 46–278 days past its partner due date, on a consistent monthly cycle (due dates falling on the 8th of each month, payoffs arriving approximately 50–280 days later). This pattern is too regular to represent random delinquency — it reflects a payment arrangement where Order extended credit to an obligor whose invoice settlement terms are structurally misaligned with the 45-day cutoff.

This obligor was excluded from Signal 4 because including it would conflate a billing-structure issue with cutoff enforcement failures. However, the underlying question is serious: Order was knowingly originating to an obligor whose payment behavior permanently exceeded the contractual threshold. Whether this was disclosed to OatFi, whether OatFi consented to a carve-out, and whether the arrangement was used to mask the true delinquency profile of the portfolio are questions that require direct examination of the servicing agreement and any side letters.
